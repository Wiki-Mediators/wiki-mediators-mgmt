# Intraday Trend-Continuation Strategies for MNQ on 5/10/30-Minute Bars: A Buildable, Empirically-Grounded Candidate Menu

## TL;DR
- **The single most important finding: continuous bar-by-bar intraday trend-following on MNQ at 5-minute resolution is the WRONG default — the only directly-relevant MNQ study (Mesfin 2026, 947 days of 5-min data) found a gross edge ceiling of just 0.07–1.50 points per trade across 14 signal families, below the ~2-point round-trip cost, while the academic "intraday momentum" literature (Gao et al.; Baltussen et al.) is actually an END-OF-DAY effect (the last 30 minutes), not a continuous trend you can ride all session.** The candidates most likely to survive push to HIGHER timeframes (10/30-min), gate entries hard by regime and time-of-day, and use multi-bar holds — exactly the structure of the one signal that DID work in Mesfin's positive control.
- **Frequency vs. concentration is a genuine vise for trend-following on MNQ.** Trend-following's documented low win rate (top trend-following CTAs typically run 38–48%; classic Turtle 55-day breakout 30–40%) and positive skew (Fung & Hsieh's lookback-straddle convexity) means few large winners carry the book — the same concentration that killed your breakout candidate. Pure 30-min systems fire too rarely (~5–10 trades/month) to ever clear Minimum Track Record Length; only 10-min breakout and time-of-day-gated 5-min pullback structures plausibly reach ~20+/month, and even those need multiple YEARS to establish a modest edge.
- **Recommendation: screen the regime-and-time-gated multi-timeframe pullback candidate (Candidate D) FIRST**, because it most closely mirrors the only empirically validated intraday MNQ structure and best balances enough-frequency against the convex-concentration trap; deprioritize pure 5-min EMA-crossover and Supertrend systems, which the evidence indicates are friction-killed chop-magnets.

## Key Findings

**1. The empirical base for intraday index-futures trend-following is far weaker than for the monthly/daily trend-following that built the CTA industry.** Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen's "Time Series Momentum" (Journal of Financial Economics, 2012) documents robust trend persistence in 58 futures over 1–12 MONTH horizons (52 of 58 contracts significant at 5%; diversified Sharpe above 1) — but this is a monthly-rebalanced phenomenon and says nothing directly about 5/10/30-minute bars. Clenow's "Following the Trend" system (50/100-day MA filter, 50-day breakout, 3×ATR trailing stop) operates on daily bars. The intraday equivalents are different animals.

**2. The famous intraday momentum results are end-of-day bets, not continuous trend-riding.** Gao, Han, Li & Zhou ("Market Intraday Momentum," JFE 2018, vol. 129(2), pp. 394–414) show the first half-hour return r1 positively predicts the LAST half-hour return r13 with "an R2 of 1.6%"; the resulting timing strategy has a "Sharpe ratio of 1.08" (versus −0.18 for Always Long) and "a high positive skewness of 0.90" — but it is one trade per day, held into the close. Baltussen, Da, Lammers & Martens ("Hedging demand and market intraday momentum," JFE 2021, vol. 142, pp. 377–403) extend this to 60+ futures, where the strategy "produces consistent returns over time, translating into high and attractive (annualized) Sharpe ratios between 0.87 and 1.73 at the asset class level," attributing it to gamma-hedging flows concentrated in the last 30 minutes. Neither validates entering on a 5-min EMA cross at 11 a.m. and trailing a stop — they validate a structural close-auction flow.

**3. MNQ-specific falsification evidence is sobering and directly on point.** Mesfin (2026, "Structural Limits of OHLCV-Based Intraday Signals in MNQ Futures," arXiv:2605.04004) tested 14 signal families on 947 days of 5-min MNQ data (Dec 2021–Aug 2025) with walk-forward validation, requiring T≥2.0, ≥30 OOS trades, positive net return after 2-point round-trip cost, and multi-year stability. **No signal passed all criteria.** Opening-range-breakout long (15-bar horizon) was the best directional momentum variant at T=1.50 — still failing. Critically: pullback entries after breakouts produced an 80.7% stop-out rate (T=−1.27); Asia-session expansion "continuation" was actively reversed (T=−10.96); and the one statistically strong momentum signal (gap-continuation short, T=3.23, +14.52 pts net) failed on FREQUENCY — only 22 trades in three years (~0.6/month). This is the exact frequency failure your first-hour candidate suffered.

**4. But edge is achievable above the OHLCV ceiling — through regime classification + multi-bar holds.** Mesfin's two positive controls cleared every criterion: an "RTH Confluence Signal" (regime label + Markov transition probability + volume z-score, entered on a 25-point ATR-scaled pullback, exited at bar 13) delivered T=5.83, +15.77 pts mean net on N=538 (OOS T=3.11, N=196, p<0.001); and a "London Session Signal B" (T=5.15, +5.77 pts, N=289). The lesson: what works is gated, multi-bar, regime-aware structure — not naive bar-by-bar trend signals.

**5. The convexity/concentration problem is structural, not a bug to engineer away.** Fung & Hsieh (Review of Financial Studies, 2001) proved trend-following payoffs replicate a lookback straddle — inherently convex, positive-skew, low-win-rate. Practitioner and CTA data corroborate: per Zaye Capital Markets' win-rate analysis, "top-performing trend-following CTAs: win rates of 38–48% are typical, with strong positive expectancy driven by large average winners," while the classic Turtle 55-day breakout system shows "win rates of 30–40%." The return distribution is right-skewed by design — analysis by CAIS of the Barclay CTA Index (January 1980–December 2022) shows "the monthly performance distribution of a typical trend-following strategy in which returns are positively skewed," i.e., "many months of small losses that are smaller in magnitude than the wins." On a single intraday instrument with no cross-sectional diversification, this means a small number of trend days will dominate P&L — precisely the concentration that killed your breakout candidate (best day = 109.5% of net profit). Trend-following on one symbol cannot diversify this away; it can only be partially mitigated by trading often enough that more trend days enter the sample.

**6. MNQ's volatility is more than large enough that friction is a small fraction of the move — IF stops are sized to timeframe.** NQ's daily ATR ran roughly 300–500 index points in 2025–early 2026 (Barchart NQ technical-analysis: 14-day ATR 461 pts, 20-day 470 pts, ≈1.5% of index; a TradingView Power Range report quoted "Session Open ATR: 436.12"). A 2-point round-trip cost is ~0.45% of one daily ATR. Opening 5-min bars commonly span tens of index points; opening 30-min bars run roughly 75–150 points; midday bars compress materially. With ATR-based stops of 1.5–3× a 5/10/30-min ATR (tens to low-hundreds of points), the 2-point cost is ~1–5% of stop distance — passing your cost-distance gate decisively on 10/30-min and acceptably on 5-min provided stops are not tighter than ~30 points. **The cost problem on MNQ is NOT stop distance — it is that the per-trade directional EDGE at 5-min resolution is smaller than the cost, per Mesfin.**

**7. Trends genuinely exist only a minority of the time, and cluster by time-of-day.** True trend days occur roughly 15–20% of sessions (about 35–40/year on the S&P; similar on Nasdaq). Within a day, the open carries a disproportionate share of price discovery: per FINRA data, "35% of daily volume occurs in the first 30 minutes and 50% occurs in the first hour," and the 30-minute opening range "captures a larger portion of daily price discovery, typically 30–35% of the session's total range." The lunch window (11:30 a.m.–1:30 p.m. ET) is documented chop where most false breakouts occur; the "power hour" (3:00–4:00 ET) re-trends as institutions position into the close. Any intraday trend system that fires uniformly across the session is structurally bleeding capital midday.

## Details

### Computable trend-continuation entry mechanisms (5/10/30-min OHLCV)

- **EMA crossover / MA stack.** Fast EMA crosses slow EMA (e.g., 9/21 on the entry timeframe); or "stacked" condition price > EMA20 > EMA50 (long). Computable from closes. On 5-min bars this fires often (multiple/day) but whipsaws badly in chop — practitioner and backtest consensus is that 1–5-min EMA crossovers generate excessive false signals. Frequency high, signal quality low.
- **Price vs. MA + MA slope.** Long only when price > EMA50 AND EMA50 slope positive over N bars. A trend FILTER more than a trigger. Cheap, robust, used as the higher-timeframe gate.
- **Donchian / channel breakout in trend direction.** Enter long on a new N-bar high (e.g., 20-bar on 10-min) only when a higher-timeframe trend filter is bullish (Clenow/turtle logic adapted intraday). Moderate frequency; captures the convex tail.
- **ADX/DMI gating.** ADX(14) measures trend STRENGTH (not direction): <20 = chop, 20–40 = trend present, >40 = strong. Use ADX>~25 and +DI>−DI (long) as a regime gate. Practitioner backtests show ADX filters improve trend-strategy profit factor and cut drawdown by suppressing range-bound entries.
- **MACD.** MACD line > signal AND histogram rising; lagging, similar profile to EMA crossover.
- **Higher-high/higher-low swing structure.** Confirm an uptrend as a sequence of HH/HL on the entry timeframe; enter on break of the prior swing high. Computable via rolling pivots.
- **Pullback-to-MA in an established trend.** In a confirmed higher-timeframe uptrend, buy when price retraces to a rising EMA20 on the lower timeframe and resumes. **Caution: Mesfin found MNQ breakout-pullback entries stopped out 80.7% of the time** — pullbacks in MNQ frequently become full reversals. Pullback entries need an independent resumption trigger, not blind limit orders at the MA.
- **Multi-timeframe alignment.** 30-min defines trend (EMA slope / price location / ADX); 5-min times entry (micro-breakout or pullback resumption). This is the classic structure and the focus of Candidate D below.

### Multi-timeframe alignment: does it help?
Practitioner consensus strongly favors a "rule of three / 4:1–6:1 ratio" top-down stack (e.g., 30-min trend → 5-min entry), claiming higher win rates when all timeframes agree and recommending standing aside on conflict. The rigorous academic evidence is thinner and indirect: the intraday momentum literature shows a longer-horizon signal (rest-of-day) predicting a shorter-horizon outcome (last 30 min), which is multi-timeframe in spirit. The study "Does Intraday Time-Series Momentum Exist in Chinese Stock Index Futures Market?" (Finance Research Letters) differs from Gao et al. (2018) by using "returns constructed at different time levels and find that the intraday time-series momentum is strongest at the 60 min level," with the first trading-session return significantly predicting the last especially when the session is defined at the 60-minute level — supporting the idea that HIGHER intraday timeframes carry more reliable directional information than 5-min. Net read: multi-timeframe filtering plausibly improves edge mainly by SUPPRESSING counter-trend and midday entries (fewer, better trades), not by adding predictive power per se. It trades frequency for quality — which is dangerous given the MinTRL frequency bar.

### Exit/bracket design preserving convexity
- **ATR trailing stop / Chandelier Exit (LeBeau).** Long stop = Highest High(N) − ATR(N)×M; default daily settings 22/3.0; backtests favor 2.5–3× ATR as the best profit-factor balance, 1.5–2× for shorter-term. Recompute ATR each bar so the stop widens as volatility rises late in trends. This is the canonical "let winners run" exit and is what preserves the convex tail.
- **The core tradeoff.** Wide trailing (3×ATR, or trail a 30-min Chandelier on a 5-min entry) captures big trend days but produces a low win rate and concentrated P&L; tight fixed targets raise win rate but CAP the few large winners that make trend-following profitable — converting a convex strategy into a negative-skew scalp (the failure mode of your tight-bracket candidate, PF 0.86). For trend-CONTINUATION you must keep the right tail: use a structural/volatility trailing stop, optionally bank partial profit at ~2R and trail the remainder.
- **Stop sizing vs. friction.** Size initial stop at 1.5–3× the entry-timeframe ATR. On 30-min (ATR ~50–150 pts) this is a 75–450-point stop; on 5-min (ATR ~15–30 pts typical) a ~30–90-point stop. The 2-point cost is 1–5% of these — friction is a small fraction of the expected move, satisfying your cost-distance lesson, so long as you do NOT trade 5-min stops tighter than ~30 points.

### Empirical verdict on whether intraday index-futures trends are tradeable
Honest synthesis: **at MONTHLY/daily horizons, time-series momentum in equity index futures is real but among the WEAKER asset classes** (MOP show equity indices positive but commodities/bonds often stronger; S&P standalone Sharpe is low). **At INTRADAY horizons, the robust documented effect is the end-of-day close-auction momentum (Gao; Baltussen), not continuous trend-riding.** For continuous 5-min trend-following specifically, the best available MNQ evidence (Mesfin) is a clean null after costs. Mean-reversion/chop dominates MNQ intraday outside of trend days and outside the open/close windows. The implication is not "give up" but "gate hard": trade trend-continuation only when a trend is plausibly underway (regime + time-of-day), accept low frequency, and push to 10/30-min where moves dwarf friction.

### Per-candidate frequency-and-concentration verdict (the menu)

**Candidate A — 30-min EMA-stack / crossover trend (single timeframe).**
Rule: long when 30-min price>EMA20>EMA50 and EMA50 slope>0 (mirror for short); Chandelier 3×ATR(22) trail; flat by session close.
Frequency: LOW — 30-min crossovers fire only a few times/week, ~5–12 trades/month. Payoff: convex, ~35–45% win rate, high concentration.
**VERDICT: FAILS the frequency bar.** At ~8/month it would need years beyond your ~950-trade MinTRL benchmark to establish a modest edge — the same trap as the first-hour candidate. Friction is negligible (huge moves) but concentration is severe. Do not screen first.

**Candidate B — 10-min Donchian breakout with 30-min trend filter + ADX gate.**
Rule: 30-min EMA50 slope defines trend; enter on 10-min 20-bar Donchian breakout in trend direction ONLY when 10-min ADX(14)>25; initial stop 2×ATR(14, 10-min); Chandelier 2.5–3×ATR trail; flat by close.
Frequency: MODERATE–HIGH — ~1–2 qualifying breakouts/day → ~20–40/month. Payoff: classic convex breakout, low win rate, concentrated but distributed across more trades than A.
**VERDICT: PASSES frequency (~20+/month) IF the ADX gate isn't too restrictive.** Main risk is the breakout-friction-ceiling Mesfin documented for ORB; the 30-min+ADX gates are the differentiator. Concentration moderate. Strong second screen.

**Candidate C — Pure 5-min EMA crossover / Supertrend (single timeframe).**
Rule: 5-min EMA9/21 cross, or Supertrend(10,3) flip; ATR stop.
Frequency: HIGH — many/day. Payoff: dominated by whipsaw; Supertrend on 5-min backtests ~42% win rate and unprofitable standalone; EMA crossovers chop-killed intraday.
**VERDICT: FAILS on edge, not frequency.** This is the friction-killed chop-magnet Mesfin's ceiling describes. Do not build.

**Candidate D — Regime-and-time-gated multi-timeframe pullback (30-min trend, 5-min entry).** *(Recommended first screen.)*
Rule: trade ONLY 9:45–11:00 ET and 3:00–4:00 ET; require 30-min trend (price>EMA20>EMA50, slope>0) AND 10-min ADX(14)>~22; enter long on a 5-min pullback to rising EMA20 confirmed by a resumption trigger (5-min close back above prior bar high) — NOT a passive limit; initial stop 1.5–2×ATR(14,5-min) (≥~30 pts); exit on bar-N time stop (~10–13 bars) or 2×ATR Chandelier trail, partial at 2R.
Frequency: MODERATE — gating to two windows on qualifying days yields ~15–30/month. Payoff: positive skew but multi-bar hold + partial banks some distribution.
**VERDICT: BEST BALANCE.** This is the closest buildable analog to Mesfin's validated RTH Confluence positive control (regime gate + ATR pullback + ~13-bar hold). Frequency is borderline-acceptable; concentration is mitigated by the time-of-day gate concentrating trades into actual trend windows. Screen this FIRST. Watch the pullback-entry stop-out risk (Mesfin's 80.7%) — the resumption-trigger requirement is essential.

**Candidate E — ADX-gated 5-min trend-continuation with Chandelier exit (no time gate).**
Rule: 10-min ADX(14)>25 and rising; enter 5-min micro-breakout in +DI/−DI direction; Chandelier 3×ATR trail.
Frequency: MODERATE–HIGH — ~20–35/month. Payoff: convex, concentrated.
**VERDICT: PASSES frequency; weaker than D** because it lacks the time-of-day gate and will take midday-chop entries that ADX alone won't fully filter. Useful as a frequency-maximizing variant if D proves too sparse.

### MinTRL reality check (applies to ALL candidates)
Bailey & López de Prado's Minimum Track Record Length formalizes how long a track record must be to establish a Sharpe above a threshold at confidence — and it LENGTHENS with negative skew and fat tails. Trend-following is positive-skew (helpful) but low win-rate and concentrated (hurtful for stability). With your prior benchmark of ~950 trades to establish a modest edge: at 20 trades/month that is ~48 months; at 30/month ~32 months. **Even the highest-frequency candidate here needs multiple years of data to statistically establish a modest intraday trend edge.** This is the central deployability constraint and argues for (a) maximizing qualified frequency without degrading edge, and (b) treating these as in-sample design hypotheses whose live confirmation will be slow.

## Recommendations
1. **Screen Candidate D first** (regime + time-of-day-gated 30-min-trend / 5-min-pullback with resumption trigger, ATR stop ≥30 pts, ~13-bar hold + 2×ATR Chandelier). It mirrors the only empirically validated intraday MNQ structure and best balances frequency against concentration. **Go/no-go threshold:** in-sample gross edge per trade must exceed ~3–4 points (comfortably above the 2-pt cost ceiling Mesfin documented) AND ≥15 trades/month AND positive net in each calendar year (Mesfin's single-year-masking failure mode is the #1 killer).
2. **Screen Candidate B second** (10-min Donchian + 30-min filter + ADX) as the frequency-robust convex breakout. **Threshold to advance:** ≥20 trades/month and after-cost profit factor >1.2 with no single day >25–30% of net profit (your concentration gate).
3. **Reject Candidate A** (too infrequent — fails MinTRL by construction) and **Candidate C** (pure 5-min EMA/Supertrend — friction-killed). Hold Candidate E as a frequency-boosting fallback only if D is too sparse.
4. **Mandatory build constraints for any candidate:** (a) execute next-bar-open with a fixed ≥2-point round-trip cost; (b) require multi-year (per-calendar-year) stability, not aggregate stats; (c) never use passive pullback limits — require a confirmation/resumption bar (Mesfin's 80.7% pullback stop-out); (d) keep the right tail (trailing/structural exit, partial-at-2R), never a fixed tight target that caps winners; (e) report trade count and run a MinTRL/Deflated-Sharpe check before believing any Sharpe.
5. **Regime/time-of-day filters to layer on all candidates:** ADX(14)>~22–25 as the cheapest trend-presence gate; restrict entries to 9:45–11:00 ET and 3:00–4:00 ET (skip 11:30–1:30 lunch chop); optionally require a volatility-regime condition (elevated VIX / expanding ATR) — Gao et al. (2018) document that intraday momentum predictability "is stronger on more volatile days, on higher volume days, on recession days, and on major macroeconomic news release days" (on FOMC release days their R² rose to 11.0% with a 20.04% annualized timing return).
6. **What would change the ranking:** if Candidate D yields <12 trades/month in-sample, widen its time windows (toward Candidate E) before abandoning trend-continuation; if 10-min breakouts (B) show the same 0.07–1.5-pt ceiling Mesfin found at 5-min, abandon breakout entries entirely and concentrate on pullback-resumption (D). If NONE of the candidates clears ~3 pts/trade gross after the regime gates, the honest conclusion is that MNQ intraday trend-continuation is not deployable and effort should redirect to the end-of-day close-auction momentum (Gao/Baltussen) structure, which has real published support.

## Caveats
- **The strongest directly-relevant evidence (Mesfin 2026) is a single arXiv/SSRN working paper by an independent researcher, not yet peer-reviewed** — but its methodology (walk-forward, pre-specified criteria, documented nulls, positive controls) is rigorous and its conclusions align with market-efficiency priors for a highly liquid instrument. Treat its 0.07–1.5-pt ceiling as a strong prior, not gospel.
- **The academic intraday-momentum papers (Gao, Baltussen) do NOT validate continuous intraday trend-following** — they validate a once-daily close-auction effect. Citing them as support for 5-min trend-riding would be a misread; they are included here precisely to mark that boundary.
- **TSMOM (Moskowitz et al.) is monthly** and equity indices are among its weaker asset classes; it provides conceptual grounding for "trends exist," not intraday tradeability.
- **Volatility figures are 2025–early-2026 and elevated** (June 2026 NQ was in a strong uptrend; ATR ~440–470 pts is above the longer-run ~1.4–1.6%-of-index norm). Single-bar 5/10/30-min point ranges are derived estimates anchored to daily ATR and the documented U-shape, not directly published averages — re-measure on your own data before sizing stops.
- **Practitioner frequency estimates here are order-of-magnitude**, derived from session structure and bar counts, not from a backtest of each exact rule set; treat them as screening priors to be confirmed empirically.
- **The concentration problem is irreducible on a single instrument.** No exit design eliminates trend-following's dependence on a few big trend days; it can only be diluted by frequency and by banking partials. Position-size accordingly and expect long flat/drawdown stretches between trend clusters.
