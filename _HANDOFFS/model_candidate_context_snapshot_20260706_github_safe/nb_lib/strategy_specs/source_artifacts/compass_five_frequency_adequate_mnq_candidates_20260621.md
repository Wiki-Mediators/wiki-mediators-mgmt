Five Distinct, Frequency-Adequate, Cost-Aware MNQ RTH Intraday Strategy Candidates
TL;DR

Build the "Noise-Area" intraday-momentum (band-breakout) strategy first. It is the only candidate that simultaneously clears both prior failure modes by design: it fires ~1.5–1.8 times per trading day (~315–450 trades/year, far above the 250/12-months bar) AND it expresses its edge over a large price distance (trailing to the close, with positively-skewed multi-point winners), so MNQ's ~1.0–1.25-point round-turn friction is a trivial fraction of the expected move. An independent practitioner replication on NQ futures produced a Sharpe of 1.67 (24.3% annualized vs. 17.6% long-only) with +6 bps expected return per trade after costs.
The cost math is the unifying design rule. For MNQ, 1 tick = 0.25 index points = $0.50 and round-turn commission is ~$1.00–$1.50, so realistic round-turn friction is roughly 1.0–1.25 index points (~$2.00–$2.50) per contract. Every candidate below is structured so the expected move is 15–60+ points, making friction a single-digit-percent drag rather than the edge-killer it was in the scalp.
The five candidates span genuinely different mechanisms — (A) intraday trend-continuation, (B) opening-range volatility breakout, (C) VWAP mean-reversion fade, (D) opening-gap fade, (E) last-hour intraday momentum — each grounded in named academic or well-documented practitioner research. Treat all five as in-sample design hypotheses; keep out-of-sample data sealed.

Key Findings

A recent direct falsification study on exactly this instrument — Mesfin (2026), Structural Limits of OHLCV-Based Intraday Signals in MNQ Futures (947 RTH days of 5-minute data, 2021–2025, arXiv 2605.04004) — tested fourteen single-bar OHLCV signal families and found that none passed strict institutional criteria after a 2-point round-trip cost, because the gross edge of next-bar-open signals is capped at roughly 0.07–1.50 points/trade. This is the quantitative confirmation of the trader's "cost-drag" lesson and the central constraint every candidate must beat. Critically, the same paper notes its positive-control signals that DID work used "regime classification and multi-bar hold periods" — i.e., edges expressed over distance and time, not single-bar scalps. That is the design template adopted here.
The strongest documented intraday effect in equity indices is intraday momentum: Gao, Han, Li & Zhou (2018, Journal of Financial Economics 129(2):394–414) show the first half-hour return predicts the last half-hour return in SPY (1993–2013), with a predictive R² of 1.6%, rising to 2.6% combining the first and twelfth half-hours, and to 3.3% when first-half-hour volatility is high. The effect is stronger on high-volatility, high-volume, recession and macro-news days. This anchors candidates A and E.
The practitioner extension — Zarattini, Aziz & Barbon (2024), Beat the Market: An Effective Intraday Momentum Strategy for SPY (Swiss Finance Institute Paper 24-97) — generalizes the "last 30 minutes" idea into an all-session "Noise Area" band-breakout that "from 2007 to early 2024... achieved a total return of 1,985% (net of costs), an annualized return of 19.6%, and a Sharpe Ratio of 1.33" (vs. 0.45 for buy-and-hold; the conditional Sharpe rises sharply in high-VIX regimes), and replicates on NQ/ES futures.
Opening-range breakout has mixed but real evidence: it is profitable in index futures in Holmberg, Rosén & Wilhelmsson (2013, on crude oil) and the "Timely ORB" study of Tsaih et al. (2019, IEEE Access, DJIA/S&P/Nasdaq/HSI/TAIEX, >8% annual returns at p<3%), but degrades out-of-sample and on naive index-futures application unless filtered by volume/volatility. Use it with a filter.
Intraday mean-reversion / overreaction-reversal is documented in US stock-index futures (Grant, Wolf & Yu 2005; Fung & Lam) and around VWAP in practitioner work; it offers the highest trade frequency.

Details
The cost-distance framework (applies to all candidates)
MNQ point value is $2.00 per index point; 1 tick = 0.25 points = $0.50. Assume ~1 tick of slippage per side (entry + exit ≈ 0.5 points = $1.00) plus ~$1.00–$1.50 round-turn commission. Total realistic round-turn friction ≈ 1.0–1.25 index points (~$2.00–$2.50) per contract. The prior scalp died because its bracket was only a few points, so friction was 30–50% of the gross edge. The rule for this batch: the structurally-motivated target must be ≥ ~15 points and the stop sized so that friction is < ~10% of the expected favorable move. At a 2026 MNQ level near 20,000–23,000, a 1% intraday move is ~200–230 points, so 15–60-point brackets are entirely realistic and make 1.25-point friction a 2–8% drag. Frequency target throughout: ≥ ~20 trades/month, ideally ~1+ trade/day so 12 months yields ≥ 250 trades.

Candidate A — "Noise-Area" Intraday Momentum (band breakout) — trend continuation, longer intraday horizon
Thesis: When MNQ breaks decisively outside a volatility-scaled equilibrium band drawn around the session open, a demand/supply imbalance is underway that tends to persist toward the close; ride it with a trailing stop.
Entry logic (computable from intraday bars):

Each minute-of-day HH:MM, compute σ(HH:MM) = the 14-day average of the absolute percentage move from that day's 9:30 open to HH:MM: σ = (1/14)·Σ|Close(t−i,HH:MM)/Open(t−i,9:30) − 1|. (Note: this is an average of absolute moves, not a standard deviation, and is time-of-day dependent.)
Gap-adjusted bands: UpperBound = max(Open_today, PrevClose)·(1 + VM·σ); LowerBound = min(Open_today, PrevClose)·(1 − VM·σ), with volatility multiplier VM = 1 and lookback = 14 (the paper's baseline; it reports 90-day lookback and VM≈1.5 as better — 90-day gives Sharpe ~1.50, VM≈1.5 gives ~1.55). concretumgroup
Decisions only on the half-hour grid (HH:00 / HH:30), first entry no earlier than 10:00. Go long if Close > UpperBound AND Close > session VWAP; go short if Close < LowerBound AND Close < VWAP.

Exit / bracket design: Trailing stop = max(UpperBound, VWAP) for longs, min(LowerBound, VWAP) for shorts, checked on the half-hour grid; close on reversal back through the band, and hard close-out at 16:00 (no overnight risk). This is a structurally large bracket: winners trail toward the close and routinely run tens of points while losers are cut near the band, producing the positive skew. Expected move: best/worst trades of +9.1%/−2.9% on SPY in the published sample — i.e., distance is enormous relative to 1.25-point friction. concretumgroup + 2
Frequency: Zarattini et al. report 7,668 trades over ~17 years ≈ 1.8 trades/day (~450/year). The Quantitativo NQ implementation confirms similar frequency. This clears the 250/12-months bar with large margin. concretumgroup
Empirical basis: Gao, Han, Li & Zhou (2018, JFE) for the underlying intraday-momentum effect; Zarattini, Aziz & Barbon (2024, SFI 24-97) for the band mechanics (1,985% total / 19.6% annualized / 1.33 Sharpe on SPY, ~43% daily hit ratio, ~2:1 payoff, positive skew +1.22, beta ≈ −0.07; performance is strongly volatility-conditional). The Quantitativo Substack replication on NQ futures (Databento minute data since 2010, modeling $0.85/contract commission + $1.40/contract fees + 0.5 tick/trade slippage) reported, with a 90-day lookback, 3% target vol and 8x cap: "Annual return is at 24.3% vs. 17.6% the benchmark; Sharpe ratio achieves 1.67 vs. 0.93 NQ long only; Max drawdown is at 24% vs. 35% the benchmark; The expected return per trade is +6 bps, with a win ratio of 38% and a payoff ratio of 2.25." Their edge test vs. non-event days returned a p-value well below 0.05.
Main risk: Low win rate (~37–43%) means long strings of small losses; the edge is convex and depends on a few large trend days, so it suffers in chop and is sensitive to the lookback/VM parameters (overfitting risk). The single-instrument, single-author provenance of the paper (not peer-reviewed in a traditional journal) warrants out-of-sample caution. Huntgathertrade

Candidate B — Opening-Range Breakout with a volatility/relative-volume filter — breakout
Thesis: On days when the first few minutes show abnormal participation, a break of the opening range marks a persistent intraday trend; an unfiltered ORB does not work, but a relative-volume / volatility-expansion filter isolates the tradeable subset.
Entry logic: Define the opening range (OR) as the high/low of the first 5 minutes after 9:30 (Zarattini's best-performing window) or first 15–30 minutes (more conservative). Compute Relative Volume = first-5-min volume ÷ trailing N-day average first-5-min volume. Enter long at the second-interval open if price closes above OR-high; short if below OR-low — only when RelVol ≥ ~1.0 (100%) or the OR width exceeds a fraction of 14-day ATR (volatility-expansion confirmation). Take the first valid breakout; one trade per day maximum to avoid noise-chasing.
Exit / bracket design: Stop at the opposite end of the opening range (the OR low for a long); profit target structured as a multiple of risk (Zarattini used 10× the entry-to-stop distance) or trail with an ATR/VWAP stop and liquidate at 16:00. Because the OR on MNQ is frequently 20–60+ points, the risk unit and the target are both far larger than 1.25-point friction; this is a wide-bracket, low-win-rate / high-payoff structure.
Frequency: One filtered trade per qualifying day. If the RelVol/volatility filter passes on the majority of days, this yields ~150–250 trades/year. This is the candidate most at risk of falling short of the 20/month bar if the filter is too strict; loosen the filter (or drop the one-trade cap to allow a second re-break) to keep frequency ≥ ~20/month.
Empirical basis: Tsaih et al. (2019, IEEE Access) "Timely Opening Range Breakout" on five index futures including Nasdaq, >8% annual returns at p<3%; Holmberg, Rosén & Wilhelmsson (2013) document intraday trending via ORB in crude-oil futures (robust in full sample, weaker in sub-periods); Zarattini, Barbon & Aziz (2024, "A Profitable Day Trading Strategy For The U.S. Equity Market," SSRN 4729284) show the unfiltered 5-minute ORB across 7,000 US stocks returned only ~3.2% annualized / 0.48 Sharpe, but restricting to high-relative-volume "Stocks in Play" produced "a total net performance of over 1,600%, with a Sharpe ratio of 2.81, and an annualized alpha of 36%" (vs. 198% for passive S&P 500) — direct evidence the filter is what makes ORB work. IEEE XploreScienceDirect
Main risk: ORB is heavily parameter-mined in the wild; many windows "work" only by overfitting (Build Alpha and QuantifiedStrategies both show naive index-futures ORB is unprofitable today). The relative-volume effect is best documented on single stocks with idiosyncratic news, not a broad index, so the filter's power on MNQ specifically is the key open question. False breakouts in low-volatility regimes are the dominant loss mode.

Candidate C — VWAP z-score mean-reversion fade — intraday mean reversion
Thesis: On range/balance days (no strong trend), MNQ price oscillates around session VWAP; when it stretches an extreme multiple of its typical deviation, it tends to snap back, and the snap-back distance is large enough to clear costs.
Entry logic: Maintain session VWAP and a rolling intraday standard deviation of (price − VWAP) (or use a Bollinger/Keltner-style band on VWAP). Compute z = (price − VWAP)/σ_dev. Fade when |z| ≥ ~2 (a 2-standard-deviation stretch) AND a trend filter says the day is not a runaway trend — e.g., ADX(14) below ~25, or price not persistently on one side of VWAP, or the day is not a large-gap/outside-range day. Short when z ≥ +2; long when z ≤ −2. Require a stall bar (a reversal candle) to avoid catching a momentum day mid-acceleration. Avoid the first ~15 minutes after the open.
Exit / bracket design: Target = reversion to VWAP (or the 1-σ band); stop = a further ~1–1.5 σ adverse (structural invalidation that the day has become a trend day). On MNQ, a 2-σ midday stretch is commonly ~0.4–0.8% (≈ 80–180 points at current index levels); even targeting only half the reversion to VWAP yields ~40–90 points, dwarfing 1.25-point friction. Win rate is typically high (60–70% in practitioner reports) with a smaller average win than the breakout strategies — but the win is still many multiples of cost.
Frequency: Two-σ touches occur on most range days, often more than once per day → roughly 1–2 trades/day (~250–450/year), comfortably clearing the bar.
Empirical basis: Documented intraday price-reversal/overreaction literature in US stock-index futures — Grant, Wolf & Yu (2005), "Intraday price reversals in the US stock index futures market: A 15-year study" (Journal of Banking & Finance 29(5):1311–1327), find "10 minutes of significant price continuation on S&P 500 Futures and then a long series of significant price reversals"; Fung & Lam (2004) and Fung, Mok & Lam on Hong Kong index futures find overnight-overreaction reversals; Lehmann (1990) and the broad short-term-reversal literature. VWAP as the reversion anchor is institutional execution-driven (funds benchmark fills to VWAP), giving the level structural significance.
Main risk: Mean reversion's catastrophic failure mode is the trend day, where price leaves VWAP and never returns — the fade keeps getting hit. The trend/ADX filter is doing the heavy lifting and is the part most likely to fail or be overfit. Tail losses can be large relative to the modest per-trade win, so stop discipline and a daily-loss cap are essential.

Candidate D — Opening-gap fade inside the prior range — gap / gap-fill
Thesis: Small overnight gaps that open inside the prior day's range tend to fill back to the prior RTH close intraday; fade the gap toward the close, but only in the high-fill-probability subset.
Entry logic: At 9:30 compute Gap = RTH open − prior RTH close, sized relative to 14-day ATR. Trade only Tiny/Small gaps (< ~0.7× ATR) that open inside the prior day's high–low range; skip large gaps (> ~1.2× ATR) and gaps that open outside the prior range (these are "gap-and-go" days that rarely fill). Enter a fade at/just after the open in the direction of the prior close (short a gap-up, long a gap-down), or use an opening-candle-continuation trigger (enter on a break back through the first 5–15-minute range edge toward the close).
Exit / bracket design: Target = the prior RTH close (the gap fill). Stop = a structural level beyond the gap extreme, e.g., 75–100% of the opening-range width beyond entry, or ~1× ATR. Because the target is the full gap (tens of points for anything but the tiniest gaps) and stops sit beyond the opening range, the bracket is wide relative to friction.
Frequency: Gaps occur every day, but the qualifying subset (small, inside-range) is perhaps 50–70% of days → ~12–17 trades/month — this is the lowest-frequency candidate and may sit just below the 20/month target. Mitigation: include both gap-up and gap-down logic, loosen the size band slightly, or add a complementary "gap-and-go" continuation rule for the outside-range days to lift count.
Empirical basis: TradingStats (2026) analyzed 2,791 NQ RTH days (2015–2025): baseline gap-fill rate 60.3% by the close, rising to 77.8% for tiny gaps opening inside the prior range and as high as ~93% under the best conditions, but falling to 8.2% for large gaps opening outside the range — direct, instrument-matched evidence that gap-size/location conditioning is decisive. SPY/QQQ studies corroborate: per SharePlanner, "about 45% of 1%–1.99% overnight gaps were fully filled on the same day. For gaps of 2% or more, the intraday fill rate drops to roughly 30%–33%." The CME ES gap-fade study (MyPivots) cautions that fixed stops eliminate the small gaps that drive profits.
Main risk: Lowest frequency of the five; the edge is concentrated in tiny gaps whose fill is reliable but whose profit (gap size) is also smallest — there is tension between gap size (profit) and fill probability. Large adverse excursions occur (one-in-ten gap fades see a 200+ point adverse move on NQ before filling, per TradingStats), so naive tight stops get whipsawed out before the fill. News-driven gaps (earnings-season macro prints, FOMC) must be excluded.

Candidate E — Last-hour intraday momentum (first-hour → close) — time-of-day / session structure
Thesis: The sign and strength of MNQ's morning return predict the direction of the last 30–60 minutes; position into the close in the direction of the established intraday trend.
Entry logic: Compute the first-half-hour (or first-hour) return r1 = Close(10:00)/Open(9:30) − 1. At ~15:00–15:30, enter in the direction of sign(r1) if |r1| exceeds a threshold (e.g., > ~0.3–0.5%, or > some ATR fraction — the effect is strongest on high-volatility, high-volume days per Gao et al.). Optionally combine with the twelfth-half-hour return (the 3:00–3:30 move) as a second predictor, as in Gao et al. (which lifts predictive R² from 1.6% to 2.6%). Hold into the close.
Exit / bracket design: Exit at 16:00 (the close). Stop = a structural intraday level (VWAP, or ~1× the last-hour ATR) to bound the downside. The last 30–60 minutes of MNQ frequently move 20–50+ points (the close is part of the U-shaped volatility smile — high open and close, quiet midday, per Andersen & Bollerslev intraday-periodicity work), so the expected distance clears friction comfortably. This is a directional, hold-to-close structure, not a scalp.
Frequency: Up to one trade per day; if filtered to days with a meaningful morning move (and/or only the higher-volatility days where the effect concentrates), ~0.5–1.0 trade/day → ~130–250/year. Trading every day on sign(r1) keeps it at ~252/year (clears the bar); filtering trades count for edge quality vs. frequency.
Empirical basis: Gao, Han, Li & Zhou (2018, JFE 129:394–414) is the canonical source — first-half-hour return positively predicts last-half-hour return, predictive R² 1.6% (2.6% combined, 3.3% on high-volatility days), statistically and economically significant, stronger on volatile/high-volume/recession/macro-news days, robust to reasonable transaction costs, and present in the Nasdaq ETF among ten others. The global intraday-time-series-momentum literature (16 developed markets) confirms it. The mechanism (Bogousslavsky 2016 infrequent-rebalancing model; late-informed traders avoiding overnight risk) is theoretically grounded. ScienceDirect
Main risk: The per-trade edge is thin (R² ~1.6% means most of the directional variance is noise), so it needs many trades and tight cost control to express; the effect has reportedly diminished over time as it became well known (some studies find intraday momentum weakening post-2010 / during COVID). End-of-day reversal at the single-stock level (the EODR literature) is the opposite effect and a reminder that index-level momentum and cross-sectional reversal coexist — direction discipline matters.
Recommendations
Build and test first: Candidate A (Noise-Area intraday momentum) on MNQ RTH. Rationale: it is the single candidate that decisively answers BOTH lessons — frequency (~1.8 trades/day → ~450/year, ~9× the underpowered first-hour strategy) and cost-distance (trailing-to-close winners of tens of points vs. ~1.25-point friction). It has the strongest documented basis (a JFE paper for the effect, a detailed practitioner paper for the mechanics, AND a futures replication on NQ specifically showing Sharpe 1.67), and the band/VWAP logic is fully specified above for direct coding.
Suggested staged plan:

Stage 1 — implement Candidate A on in-sample MNQ RTH data with the paper's baseline parameters (14-day lookback, VM=1, half-hour decision grid, VWAP+band trailing stop, 16:00 flat). Pre-register the cost model (1.0–1.25 pts round-turn). Go/no-go thresholds: ≥ 250 trades over 12 months; profit factor ≥ ~1.2 after costs; positive expectancy per trade ≥ ~2–3× friction (i.e., ≥ ~3–4 points net); positive skew preserved. If it passes, proceed; if win rate or payoff collapses after costs, inspect whether the half-hour grid (which limits overtrading) is intact.
Stage 2 — only if A passes — add Candidate C (VWAP mean-reversion fade) as a complementary, negatively-correlated sleeve (mean-reversion profits in the chop where momentum bleeds). Benchmark: does combining A+C smooth the equity curve (lower drawdown, higher combined Sharpe) without either sleeve individually failing the 250-trade / cost-distance tests?
Stage 3 — Candidate B (filtered ORB) as a third diversifier, but first validate that the relative-volume / volatility filter actually fires ≥ ~20×/month on MNQ; if not, it fails Lesson 1 before you even test edge.
Defer Candidates E and D. E's edge is statistically thin and reportedly decaying; treat it as a low-conviction overlay or a conditioning filter on A rather than a standalone. D is the lowest-frequency candidate (likely ~12–17 trades/month) and should only be pursued if you widen its criteria to clear 20/month.

Thresholds that would change the ranking: If in-sample Candidate A shows < 250 trades/year or a post-cost profit factor < 1.1, demote it and promote C (highest frequency, highest win rate). If the ORB relative-volume filter proves to fire frequently AND profitably on MNQ, promote B above C. If gap-fade D can be widened to ≥ 20 trades/month while preserving the inside-range fill edge (>70%), it becomes a strong high-win-rate diversifier.
Caveats

Everything here is an in-sample design hypothesis. Keep out-of-sample data sealed; do not tune parameters on it. Several cited results (Zarattini papers, Quantitativo replication, TradingStats) are practitioner/non-peer-reviewed and single-instrument, so they are vulnerable to overfitting and selection bias — use them to motivate structure, not to set expectations.
The Mesfin (2026) MNQ falsification result is the sobering counterweight: on this exact instrument, fourteen naive single-bar OHLCV signal families failed after a 2-point cost. The candidates here are deliberately designed to differ from those failures (multi-bar holds, regime/volatility filters, distance-based brackets), but there is no guarantee the same efficiency that killed the single-bar signals will not erode these too. Budget for the possibility that the net edge after MNQ costs is small.
Parameter sensitivity and regime dependence are pervasive: momentum (A, B, E) needs trending/volatile regimes; mean-reversion (C, D) needs balance/range regimes. None will work in all regimes; a volatility-regime switch (e.g., VIX or realized-vol gating) may be necessary and is itself a fitting risk.
Intraday effects decay. Gao et al.'s momentum and many time-of-day anomalies have reportedly weakened since their discovery and during COVID; recent data may show smaller effects than the historical studies.
Cost realism: the 1.0–1.25-point friction assumes good fills on a liquid contract; widen it for fast markets, the open, and macro releases. Slippage on stop exits during volatility expansion (the ORB and mean-reversion failure modes) can exceed the modeled tick.
News/event exclusions: FOMC, CPI/NFP, and earnings-season macro prints distort gaps, opening ranges, and reversion — consider excluding or down-sizing around scheduled events.
Correlated-instrument / session notes: Candidate A replicates on ES/MES (Quantitativo's blended ES+NQ portfolio reached Sharpe ~1.57) and could be paper-tested there for confirmation without changing infrastructure. Candidate C's reversion edge is reportedly stronger in the thinner pre-RTH (Globex) hours per practitioner backtests, but that would breach the RTH-only constraint and should be treated as a separate future experiment, not part of this batch.
